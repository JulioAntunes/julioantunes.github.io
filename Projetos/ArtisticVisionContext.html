Introduction

Emigration is often discussed in terms of its financial and social impacts on individuals and local societies. However, the emotional and psychological dimensions of emigration, particularly the experiences of family separation, forced displacement, and trauma, remain underexplored. This essay seeks to unravel these less-discussed aspects, highlighting how they affect individual development, especially with a focus on the cognitive and social development of children.

To answer the central research question – "How do the emotional and psychological dimensions of emigration, specifically related to family separation, forced displacement, and trauma, influence a child's cognitive and social development?" – this study follows a structured exploration. First, it references articles and indirect sources to address the broader context of poverty's impact on children's cognitive and social development (National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Early Child Care Research Network, 2005), the importance of parental influence on emotional and psychological development (Wilson, 2019), and the effects of forced displacement, social catastrophes, traumas, and counterproductive environments on individuals (Iraklis, 2021; Choi et al., 2021).

Subsequently, the essay navigates into the mental health implications of emigration, drawing on insights from the World Health Organization (Mental Health and Forced Displacement, 2021) and a study published in PLOS Medicine (Blackmore et al., 2020). This section aims to highlight the prevalence of mental health disorders among migrants and refugees and emphasizes the need for comprehensive approaches to address their mental health needs.

Following this, the discussion expands to the challenges encountered throughout the migration journey, exploring obstacles ranging from limited opportunities and violence before migration to difficulties in healthcare access and family separation after reaching a new place (World Health Organization, 2021; Bempong et al., 2019). By addressing these challenges, the essay aims to underscore the mental health impact of the stressors encountered during the migration process.

The subsequent section explores the impact of poverty on family dynamics and child development, drawing on the National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Early Child Care Research Network's research (2005). This research categorizes families based on their poverty status and analyzes the long-term effects, emphasizing the need for targeted support for families facing prolonged economic challenges.

Finally, the essay navigates the debate on nature versus nurture in personal development during family separation and forced displacement. While highlighting the perspective of Robert Plomin, who argues for the predominant role of genetics (Wilson, 2019), it also considers the environment's impact on child health within immigrant families (Choi et al., 2021). The varying perspectives presented in this section contribute to a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing personal development.

In essence, each section of this essay contributes to answering the central research question by exploring different dimensions of the emotional and psychological aspects of emigration. This structured approach allows for a comprehensive examination of the topic, providing insights into the multifaceted challenges faced by individuals and families undergoing emigration experiences.

Emigration and Mental Health

Emigration can have a significant impact on mental health. According to the World Health Organization (Mental Health and Forced Displacement, 2021), refugees, asylum seekers, and irregular migrants are of special concern and need protection and support.

Migrants and refugees can be exposed to various stress factors that affect their mental health and well-being before, during, and after their migration journey, as well as during their settlement and integration. The prevalence of common mental disorders, such as depression, anxiety, and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), tends to be higher among migrants exposed to adversity and refugees than among host populations.

A study published in PLOS Medicine (Blackmore et al., 2020) found that the prevalence of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) was 31.46% among refugees and asylum seekers. Many migrants and refugees lack access to mental health services or face barriers in obtaining them. Additionally, they encounter disruptions in the continuity of care.

Addressing the mental health needs of migrants and refugees should involve organizing inclusive and accessible promotion and prevention programs, strengthening mental health as part of general health services, and ensuring timely diagnosis, treatment, and rehabilitation (Blackmore et al., 2020).

As stated by the World Health Organization (2021), "Migrants and refugees contribute positively to society, but they cannot reach their full potential unless they are in good physical and mental health." (Mental Health and Forced Displacement, 2021).

Challenges Across the Migration Journey

Migrants and refugees face a range of challenges throughout their migration journey, as discussed by the World Health Organization (Mental Health and Forced Displacement, 2021). Before migrating, they often struggle with limited job opportunities, lack of education access, and exposure to violence, conflict, poverty, or persecution.

During the journey, they encounter dangerous conditions, violence, and difficulties accessing essential services. After reaching a new place, obstacles persist, including barriers to healthcare, uncertain legal status, family separation, and low living conditions (Bempong et al., 2019).

The integration phase brings a specific set of challenges, including poor living and working conditions, unemployment, cultural assimilation difficulties, and discrimination (Iraklis, 2021). The report highlights the mental health impact of these stressors, emphasizing the need for attention to the diverse challenges faced by migrants and refugees (Alegría et al., 2021).

Impact of poverty on family dynamics and child development

These people who emigrate do not just suffer from the migration process, but their background of poverty in their home countries creates traumas that influence their development in certain aspects, especially in children (National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Early Child Care Research Network, 2005).

The National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Early Child Care Research Network conducted research on children's cognitive and social development influenced by poverty. It shows how different experiences of poverty influence children's development over time (National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Early Child Care Research Network, 2005).

The study categorized families into four groups based on their poverty status: never-poor, early-poor, late-poor, and always-poor. The researchers used two models for analysis. In Model 1, they observed that families in persistent poverty faced challenges, with the always-poor group providing the least enriching home environments. For instance, the never-poor families scored highest in-home enrichment (overall quality and resources available within the home environment), while the always-poor families scored lowest. However, Model 2, which considered additional factors like maternal education and depressive symptoms, revealed a more detailed and complex reality (National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Early Child Care Research Network, 2005).

The differences in home enrichment were partially explained by these factors (maternal education and depressive symptoms), indicating that the initial disparities reduced when accounting for certain variables. Importantly, the study found that persistent poverty, especially during early childhood, was associated with lower cognitive and social development in children. For instance, children in always-poor families had the lowest scores, showing the long-term impact of poverty on cognitive skills. This means that, in school, these children have more difficulties adapting and developing, and that reflects on their future educationally and professionally (National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Early Child Care Research Network, 2005).

The research underscored the significance of considering various factors when studying the effects of poverty on child development. It emphasized the need for targeted support for families facing prolonged economic challenges. In conclusion, it posits that children are notably affected by poverty from a young age, with consideration given to factors such as maternal education and depressive symptoms. This can result in enduring consequences, as these children may encounter difficulties throughout their lives, especially in their academic and professional pursuits, owing to the less developed cognitive aspects compared to their counterparts (National Institute of Child Health and Human Development Early Child Care Research Network, 2005).

The Role of Parenting and Genetics in Personal Development During Family Separation and Forced Displacement

Robert Plomin, interviewed by Clare Wilson, suggests that how we raise our children may not have a significant impact on their eventual personalities and success (Wilson, 2019). According to him, parenting has minimal influence on a child's development, as personality tends to develop independently, and parents cannot change it. Plomin emphasizes that genetics has a more crucial and measurable influence on our lives.

While the environment certainly matters, studies on personality reveal that even identical twins raised apart share similar personalities. For instance, the case of Jack Yufe and Oskar Stohr, identical twins separated as babies due to their parents divorce, demonstrates the enduring impact of genetics. Despite being raised in vastly different environments—Yufe in Trinidad and Stohr in Germany, where he became involved with the Nazi movement—they exhibited striking similarities (Dahl, 2015). Despite their contrasting upbringings and environments, the twins similarities, uncovered during their participation in the Minnesota Twin Family Study conducted by William G. Iacono and Matt McGue, highlight the enduring influence of genetics on various aspects of personality behavior, intelligence, and other traits that had long been thought to be mainly shaped by the environment (Iacono & McGue, 2002).

The effects of the environment, as highlighted by Plomin are considered random, underscoring that inherited differences constitute the major systematic force shaping our identities (Wilson, 2019). Despite being Plomin’s perspective, research on the significance of the environment navigates into the effects of neighborhood environments on child health within immigrant families, utilizing nationally representative individual, family, and community datasets (Choi et al., 2021). In this context, this research found that the neighborhood environment significantly influences the physical and socioemotional health of children from immigrant families. This impact persists even after accounting for parents’ demographic characteristics, socioeconomic status, acculturation, and healthcare issues (Choi et al., 2021).

Notably, systematic efforts to pinpoint environmental influences have encountered challenges. For instance, the act of parents reading to their children, often presumed to influence the likelihood of reading, may itself be influenced by the parents genetic predisposition for reading. Plomin argues that altering a child's personality, particularly in addressing behavioral issues, poses a formidable challenge for parents. He emphasizes that exam results find their best predictor in a polygenic score for educational achievement, derived from an array of genetic tests.

In conclusion, Plomin advocates for a shift in parental perspective, urging them to perceive themselves as resource managers. The emphasis lies in discovering their children's interests and providing opportunities rather than attempting to mold them as if they were malleable clay (Wilson, 2019).

In the documentary film "Three Identical Strangers" (Three Identical Strangers Study - What We Know about the Experiment That Separated Twins at Birth, 2020), a real-life account unfolds about triplets separated at birth and coincidentally reunited as adults. The film discusses the ongoing nature versus nurture (the interplay between genetic factors and environmental influences) debate and addresses the ethical concerns surrounding a clandestine study that caused their separation. Despite growing up in distinct environments, the triplets exhibited shared interests and specific similarities in various aspects of their lives, including food preferences, cigarette brand choices, wrestling abilities, careers in the restaurant industry, challenges with mental health, and even their political perspectives.

In contrast to the viewpoint mentioned earlier, the podcast "Creating a Family," which explores the influence of genetics and environment on individual and child development, argues that the environment in which we are raised holds greater significance than genetics (Show, 2015). According to their discussions, ongoing studies on adopted twins from diverse families suggest variations in development based on the family environment. However, it is essential to note that this research is still ongoing, and a definitive conclusion regarding this perspective is yet to be reached. This differs from opposing statements that assert a more predominant role for genetics in shaping outcomes.

In conclusion, exploring various sources reveals the complexity of understanding how parenting and family environments shape child development, especially in the context of emigration. While existing research offers valuable insights, there's a need for more extensive studies with broader data beyond specific situations. This is particularly crucial when examining the impact of emigration on a child's development, especially when separated from the family at a young age.

In relation to my Audiovisual Installation, these research findings strongly support the themes regarding the psychological effects of emigration. The necessity to explore these topics arises from a desire to confidently present them in the installation, ensuring that the depicted themes align authentically with the reality of emigration's influence on child development. This research not only validates the ideas presented but also underscores their significance in conveying an accurate narrative within the overarching theme of the Audiovisual Installation.

Conclusion

In summary, emigration extends its profound impacts beyond the commonly discussed financial and social aspects, encompassing mental health, challenges throughout the migration journey, and the influential role of poverty on family dynamics and child development.

The migration journey itself, marked by limited opportunities, violence, and barriers to support, significantly affects individual’s mental well-being, leading to concerns like depression and anxiety. This underscores the critical need for comprehensive research to better understand the complexities surrounding the mental health implications of emigration.

The ongoing debate about how genetic predisposition interacts with environmental influences becomes particularly relevant when studying the effects of emigration on personal development. While some argue for the importance of genetics, others emphasize the role of the environment. Studies on adopted twins reveal diverse outcomes, with some experiencing distinct life trajectories, while others share remarkably similar lives despite differing environments. This highlights the ongoing need for extensive research to unravel the intricate interplay of genetics and environment in the context of emigration.

Considering the impact of poverty on family dynamics and child development, especially in the context of emigration, is crucial. Children, often affected by poverty from a young age, face enduring consequences, leading to academic and professional challenges throughout their lives. Factors such as maternal education and depressive symptoms add nuance to understanding the less developed cognitive aspects of these children compared to others.

As we navigate the layers of emigration, this exploration reveals a demand for comprehensive approaches and interventions to assist individuals and families immersed in these experiences. The central research question guiding this study: "How do the emotional and psychological dimensions of emigration, specifically related to family separation, forced displacement, and trauma, influence a child's cognitive and social development?", drives our understanding of the psychological aspects of emigration.

This research contributes to elucidating the emigrant experience from a psychological standpoint, examining the impact of parent separation and the influence of poverty on a child's cognitive development. The complexities revealed through this inquiry emphasize the urgency of ongoing research to better support those facing the multifaceted challenges of emigrating to new places.
